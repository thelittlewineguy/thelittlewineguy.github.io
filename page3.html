Comparing Data Collected

Political

SB 1

 
This California tax increase on gas and vehicle registration implemented in 2018 will make shipping wine via diesel trucks, the predominate method in California, more expensive. However, the government funding will predominately go to improving the road infrastructure wine transporters are most likely to use. $5 billion annually is being invested in infrastructure, and in the 2018 fiscal year rail is only receiving 0.5% of the funding.
 
Threat of Federal Government in Reducing Emissions Standards
 
Under the Trump administration, the federal government has consistently weakened environmental regulations. This will be a constant threat to California’s green initiatives. This is most recently evidenced by the EPA’s attempts to lower vehicle tailpipe standards in California.
 

Immigration Policy


As discussed in the overview of the US System, US immigration policy has a significant impact on the number of migrant workers transported to the US and available for harvest. The lower number resulted in labor shortages, which had wide ranging impacts.


Minimum Wage Policy/Overtime


Minimum wage policy and forthcoming changes to overtime rules in California also have an impact on the transportation of people as farmers are looking ahead to the impact these changes will have on their cost structure. The impact of these changes (particularly when coupled with the labor shortages) are pushing many to consider changing crops from grapes to almonds or olives, which require much less labor or moving toward mechanization.

Freight Transportation is Critical to California’s GDP

 

California’s economy is bolstered by freight transportation and the state is America’s largest hub of domestic commerce and international trade.

· Freight movement generates $740 billion, which is roughly a third of California’s $2.2 trillion economy.

· Over 5 million California jobs are in freight-dependent industry


Economies of Scale in California’s Distribution Laws


Both the Three-Tier and Direct Sales to Retail model are dominated by large companies. Recent consolidation within this distributor level in the three-tier model results in transportation efficiencies. To illustrate consolidation impact within this distribution model, revenues at the nation’s three wine distributors equals half of the U.S. Wine Market Value. In the Direct Sales to Retail Model, these wine companies have the scale to transport their own goods within California. There are opportunities to incentivize environmental initiatives with these few, large players in California wine distribution in.

Waste from lost productivity


 The labor shortage resulting from the change in immigration policy, which has resulted in the loss of labor force in parts of California (both because of a general shortage of labor because there are fewer workers and because workers are being transported to higher wage areas in Napa and Mendocino (Cannabis)), firms have skipped productive activities, been unable to harvest crops, elected to pull out productive crops and let land lie fallow due to cost of harvesting and taken other actions that have resulted in significant waste because either the cost of labor was higher than the benefit (profit) from their crop or there simply was no labor to be found due to labor shortages.


“False” Minimum Wage


Farm workers are commuting 2-3 hours each way to work due to a variety of factors including high cost of living in the areas where they are working and the higher wages and benefits earned. They are not commuting because of lack of available work in their communities. [They are not paid for commute time, which means that the effective wage paid assuming $19.50 wage/ 8-hour work day and 4-hour round trip commute, effective hourly wage is actually $13.00. This worker is not only not getting paid overtime for the hours worked after 8 hours, he or she is not getting paid at all.] [CONFIRM]


Loss of Efficiency


The loss of experienced labor force in Napa, move to busing laborers in from other areas means that vineyard managers are retraining farmworkers who don’t have experience in grapes. This has led to a loss of efficiency – it takes a longer time for an inexperienced laborer to complete the same task than it would for an experienced laborer (Quackenbush).


Social

Wine

People

Focus of Sustainable Winegrowing, Not Transportation

 

Much of the consumer and industry awareness focuses on best environmental practices from a winegrowing perspective. Greenhouse gas emissions from transportation is not in the public consciousness, and is an unpleasant reality wine companies gloss over when explaining sustainability initiatives.

Loss of Social welfare


Some workers are commuting 4-6 hours a day to work in Napa as a result of the labor shortage and the fact that Napa is paying higher wages and providing benefits than other areas of California. This is completely lost non-productive time that is being removed both from productive economic time and completely disrupts the family structure. A worker interviewed noted that he leaves his home in Stockton to get in a van at 4:40 a.m, he is on a Napa vineyard at 6:30 a.m, 12 hours later his is home and see his kids faces and “gives them a hug before turning in at 9:30 p.m… which is hard for me as a man and a father” (Kitroeff and Mohan), but notes that work in Napa is better because he makes $19.50 an hour instead of $14.75 and in Napa “they treat you differently… they don’t pressure you, they respect the law” (Kitroeff and Mohan). As noted above, if this worker is not paid for his commute time he is actually making $13.00 an hour in Napa since he is commuting at least 4 hours a day.

California Lagging in Adoption of Lower Emission Commercial Trucking Technology

 

The Diesel Technology Forum shows that approximately 23% of California's commercial heavy-duty diesel trucking fleet – the largest in the United States at nearly a million vehicles – uses the newest, cleanest diesel technology. The national average is 30% adoption.


Rail Technology Lacking

 

Wine shipping by train is primarily being used for transportation from California to states on East Coast; an east-west route. However, there are opportunities to use north-south routes within California’s existing infrastructure.


Future Innovation in Electric Trucking

 

Tesla is developing electric semis with ranges of 200 to 300 miles. There may be opportunities for California to incentivize adoption of this technology.

Mechanization


Mechanization is one of the biggest technological innovations that could change the face of labor in Napa. Many vineyard owners/managers are investing in mechanization equipment to reduce need for labor and changing the footprint of vineyards to allow for increased mechanization. (Adams, Quackenbush).


Alternative Transportation


Trains! Opening a fast, environmentally efficient public transportation link from Napa to the Central Valley/San Joaquin valley would significantly change the face of labor transportation. This would require significant time and infrastructure (billions of dollars) but could reduce the commute significantly.

Transportation in the U.S. and Greenhouse Emissions

 

Transportation accounts for 27% of the United States greenhouse gas emissions when analyzed by sector. According to a National Geographic study, if you live in New York, it may be more environmentally friendly to buy wine sent by container ship from Bordeaux to a port in New Jersey than to buy California Wine trucked across the country in diesel truck.

Carbon Footprint


As a result of a combination of factors including the labor shortage, high cost of housing in Napa and lack of public transportation infrastructure workers are commuting to Napa from lower cost areas to work in Napa. They receive higher wages and more benefits in Napa (a positive) at the cost of a 2-3 hour commute each way. Some workers are commuting on their own by car and labor contractors are bringing workers in by the bus or vanload. The monthly carbon footprint of this commute is anywhere from 2,681 lbs to 10,311 lbs, as set out in more detail in the discussion of US Systems. This is per worker (or van/busload) and is not offset in any manner.


Mechanization

Mechanization can save as much as 25-75% of the cost for individual task and can be employed for labor intensive tasks in vineyards such as pruning, trimming sucker shoots, removing leaves and thinning shoots and grape clusters (Quackenbush). Viewing mechanization based on integrated systems-based approach offers many opportunities for expansion, but is still in early stages of experimentation and is expensive (Adams). Farmers may not only have to change the layout of farms to make room for mechanization, but also the variety of fruit harvest to withstand mechanical harvesting (Mohon, discussing raising grapes).


Lost Land Productivity-


The labor shortage has had a direct result on land productivity on several level, including:

• Farmers have been unable to harvest crops – entire fields have been left unharvested and left to rot

• “Vast areas of the Central Valley have switched from labor intensive crops such as grapes or vegetables to almonds, which are mechanically shaken from the tree” (Mohan).

The Transportation Impact of Wine Packaging

Wineries can use less glass in their bottle that results in shipping less mass and making transportation more efficient as well as environmentally friendly. According to California Sustainable Winegrowing, switching to bag-in-the-box packaging could reduce the carbon wine footprint by 40%.

Alcohol Distribution Governed by State Laws

In the United States, the environmental impact of alcohol distribution is influenced by legislation in individual states. Across all industries’ distribution models, factors like greenhouse gas emissions caused by trucking or tax credits for solar panels at a warehouse all vary state to state. California leads the country in environmental standards that impact transportation.

Minimum Wage and Overtime Laws


As discussed above, and in the US Systems Overview, CA Minimum wage is $10.50. Minimum wage will increase to $15 by 2023. California currently requires that employers pay farmworkers overtime after they have worked 10 hours, but new overtime rules phasing in starting in 2019 will require overtime pay after 8 hours (Ulloa and Myers). While many vineyard managers/owners in Napa already pay more than $15 per hour for labor, the increase in minimum wage and overtime rules is pushing some farmers to move towards mechanization and/or shift crops to crops that require less labor (Quackenbush).


H-2A Visa Requirements


One means for employing workers from outside the United States for agricultural purposes is through the H-2A visa program. This program allows employers to hire workers for up to 10 months and requires that employers provide housing, transportation (from home country) and make a good faith effort to hire American workers for position (Mobley). This program is underutilized in the wine industry because it is administratively burdensome and difficult to administer (Mobley), but provides wineries willing to make the investment a way to get workers to Napa. Note that even if Napa elects to move toward H-2A visas, these can be difficult to obtain, in 2016, only 134,000 H-2A Visas were issued nationwide and California had an estimated 400,000 farmworkers (Quackenbush).
