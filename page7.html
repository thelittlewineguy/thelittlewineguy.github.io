Two Distribution Models' Impact on Transporting Wine Within California

1) Three-Tier Model

In America’s alcohol distribution, most states are governed by a three-tier model where the supplier sells to a state-level distributor who then sells to retailers. In this supply-chain also utilized in California, the wine is stored in three different locations and transported two times before it reaches a store shelf or wine list. In the three-tier system a few, large distributorships control a significant market-share of California’s wine sales and have profit motivations in creating the most efficient routes that consume the least fuel. 

2) Direct-to Retailer Sales Model…A Tale of Scale for Wineries 

California is unique in that it allows in-state wineries to sell directly to retailers in restaurants. This is a tale of extreme scales. Small wine producer, who emit less greenhouse gases can service their retail customers. Conversely, the largest sales and production wine companies like Gallo, Jackson Family, Treasury use this distribution model to manage their sales strategies and create cost efficiencies in transportation.


SB 1 Impacts Wine Transportation

California began 2018 implementing the Road Repair and Accountability Act also known as SB 1. This state laws use gas taxes to prioritize to improving roads over rail infrastructure. The law invests $5 billion annually into California Transportation Infrastructure.

Starting in 2018 the diesel fuel excise tax will increase from 20 cents to 36 cents per gallon. And the diesel sales tax will increase from 9% to 12%


Rail will only receive $40.8 million in 2018, versus Compared to $1.15 billion for State Highways and $1.15 billion for local streets and roads.

Wine Transportation’s Dependence on Diesel

California’s wine is primarily transported by diesel trucks and the state is lagging in adopting lower emission commercial trucking technology. The distributor and trucking companies responsible for transporting wine add a fuel surcharge to shipments and cost of recent tax increases will be shared amongst transporter, customers, and the wine consumer.


California ranks 47th out of 50 in the United States in adopting the latest low emissions commercial truck technology.

Limits in Rail Infrastructure Make Train Shipping Not Viable

Using the latest rail data, between 2003 and 2013 17 million tons of California wine was transported via rail. This amounts to roughly $72 million worth of greenhouse gases eliminated at current California cap-and-trade levels. However in 2016, California produced over 680 million gallons of wine. This shows the tremendous potential to cut greenhouse emissions if infrastructure investments are made. 

Pros

· Compared to truck only shipping, rail offers significant cost and environmental advantages.


Cons

· Transit time two to four times greater than truck-only solutions; this increases time that wine could be exposed to increased temperatures

· Significant volume is required for cost-effectiveness.

· Insulated box cars are costly and may not be effective in high temperature situations; innovation is needed.
